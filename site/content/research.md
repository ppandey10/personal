## Aging and Longevity

Over the past few months, I have been working with [Dr. Mark Olenik](https://donertas-group.github.io/author/mark-olenik/) and [Dr. Melike Dönertaş](https://www.leibniz-fli.de/research/research-groups/doenertas) on a project aimed at understanding the stochastic nature of aging.

Chronological age simply measures the time elapsed since birth. Aging, however, refers to the progressive decline in biological function that increases vulnerability to disease and death. Quantifying this process, often described as estimating an individual’s **biological age**, is central to understanding age-related diseases and designing interventions that promote healthy longevity. One widely used framework for this purpose is the concept of an **aging clock**.

A key open question in the development of aging clocks is whether aging is fundamentally deterministic or whether it emerges from stochastic, cumulative molecular changes over time[^1]. Our work addresses this question by developing a dynamical model of DNA methylation that explicitly incorporates stochasticity. The goal is to understand how variability accumulates across CpG sites and how this accumulation gives rise to measurable age acceleration in individuals.

By combining mechanistic modeling with statistical learning approaches, we aim to bridge the gap between molecular-level noise and population-level aging patterns.

## MSc Thesis

My MSc thesis focused on gravitational-wave data analysis from compact binary mergers using Bayesian inference.

Extracting physical parameters from a detected gravitational-wave (GW) signal requires sampling the posterior probability distribution of the source parameters. This process depends critically on accurate waveform models that simulate the signal generated by a compact binary system. In practice, analyzing a single GW event may require generating on the order of ten million waveforms, making computational efficiency a central challenge.

This challenge will become even more pressing with next-generation detectors, which are expected to observe significantly more events than current facilities. Improving waveform generation speed is therefore essential for maximizing the scientific output of future large-scale GW experiments.

A promising strategy for acceleration is the construction of machine learning–based surrogate models. While extensive work has been done for binary black hole systems, comparatively fewer studies focus on binary neutron stars—particularly when including higher-order modes and precession effects.

In my thesis, I developed `mlgw_bns_HOM`, a neural-network-based surrogate model designed to accelerate the generation of Effective-One-Body (EOB) waveforms for binary neutron stars. The model incorporates higher-order modes (HOMs) and precession effects to ensure accuracy and generalization across parameter space. By combining data compression techniques with analytical structure from the waveform model, the approach achieves substantial computational speed-ups while maintaining high fidelity.
